Building a Stronger Dominion: A Mission for Canadian Renewal

Canada produces world-class talent and sits on globally significant resources‚Äîyet struggles to convert these inputs into strategic capacity. A focus on two reinforcing pillars, Talent and Energy, could reverse the drift.

Read more

The Canadian Moment

Something shifted in American venture capital around 2020. A generation of investors who came up funding consumer apps and enterprise software started writing checks for defense contractors, nuclear startups, and semiconductor fabs. They called it "American Dynamism"‚Äîa bet that the most important companies of the next decade would build physical things that mattered for national security and industrial capacity.

The thesis wasn't subtle: great power competition is back, supply chains are strategic, and the country that makes things will have leverage over the country that merely consumes them.

Canada watched this happen. We've been politely interested.

That's no longer sufficient. Rising geopolitical tensions, fracturing supply chains, and the global race for technological sovereignty demand a distinctly Canadian response. Not a copy of American ideas‚Äîwe lack the defense budget and venture ecosystem to simply replicate what Andreessen Horowitz is doing. But a response that captures Canadian strengths while confronting Canadian weaknesses.

That response rests on two pillars: Talent (mobilizing our people) and Energy (leveraging our power).

These aren't abstract categories. They're the two areas where Canada has genuine global advantage‚Äîand where we've been most complacent about squandering it. We produce elite engineers, then wave goodbye as they board flights to San Francisco. We sit on critical minerals the world desperately needs, then ship raw ore for others to refine into value.

A serious national strategy would treat these as reinforcing assets. Ambitious energy projects attract ambitious people. Talented people drive innovation in energy and resources. The loop compounds‚Äîor, in our case, it could, if we chose to make it.

This is pragmatic yet aspirational. Call it a vision for national resilience. Call it building a stronger dominion.

Pillar 1: Talent‚ÄîMobilizing Our People

The Brain Drain Problem

Canadian universities punch far above their weight. Waterloo's computer science program rivals MIT and Stanford. The University of Toronto helped pioneer modern deep learning. Our engineering schools feed talent into the world's most demanding technical organizations.

Then those graduates leave.

The pattern is so familiar it barely registers as a problem anymore. A bright engineer finishes her degree at McGill, accepts an offer from Google or Meta, relocates to the Bay Area, and never comes back. She might retain a vague fondness for Canada‚Äîsomewhere to visit family at Christmas‚Äîbut her productive years, her taxes, her startup ideas, and eventually her own hiring decisions all happen somewhere else.

The numbers are stark. Software engineers in San Francisco earn 1.5‚Äì2x their Canadian counterparts, even adjusting for cost of living. The most interesting work‚Äîthe frontier AI labs, the ambitious hardware companies, the defense tech startups tackling hard problems‚Äîclusters in the U.S. Canada becomes a farm system, developing talent for others to deploy.

This isn't inevitable. It's a policy choice we've made through inaction.

The Defense Tech Opportunity

A thriving defense technology sector would change the calculus. Not because patriotism alone keeps people home‚Äîit doesn't‚Äîbut because ambitious engineers want to work on hard problems that matter. Missile guidance systems. Autonomous drones. Satellite constellations. Cyber defense at nation-state scale. These are genuinely interesting technical challenges, and they're currently being solved almost entirely in the United States.

Canada has more latent capacity here than most people realize. Our startups submitted more applications to NATO's DIANA innovation accelerator than any other country in its first cohort. Companies like MDA have built world-class space technology for decades. Reaction Dynamics in Montreal is developing rockets. The talent and ambition exist.

What's missing is the ecosystem to support them.

American defense tech founders have access to specialized accelerators like AFWERX and In-Q-Tel, a CIA-backed venture fund that has seeded dozens of successful companies. They have a Department of Defense willing to act as early customer, providing revenue while products mature. They have venture capitalists who understand the sector's longer timelines and are willing to underwrite them.

Canadian founders get none of this. Our venture capital community‚Äîhistorically cautious, oriented toward capital-efficient SaaS and fintech‚Äîviews defense and hardware startups as too risky, too slow, too weird. Government procurement systems are designed to minimize risk and controversy, not cultivate domestic capability. The result is a structural disadvantage that talent and ambition alone can't overcome.

Fixing this requires deliberate intervention: a Canadian defense technology accelerator with real funding and mentorship, procurement pathways that let startups sell to DND without drowning in bureaucracy, and venture capital willing to take defense seriously. When our best founders see genuine support for building in Canada first, they will.

Building Dual-Fluency Professionals

Defense technology doesn't just need engineers. It needs bridge-builders‚Äîpeople fluent in both technology and the language of defense, government, and national security.

These hybrid professionals are rare and valuable. A reservist with a computer science background who understands both military doctrine and machine learning architectures. An aerospace engineer who has spent time consulting with the Air Force and grasps how procurement actually works. A policy expert who can translate between uniformed personnel and startup founders.

This connective tissue barely exists in Canada. We've siloed our technical community from our defense community so thoroughly that they often can't communicate effectively, let alone collaborate.

Building this capacity means creating pathways: education programs that combine engineering with security studies, exchange mechanisms between DND and the tech sector, and cultural shifts that encourage respect between uniformed and civilian innovators. It means treating "dual fluency" as a skill set worth cultivating, not an accident that occasionally happens.

Evolving Ontario's Manufacturing Base

Southern Ontario's manufacturing heritage is both asset and vulnerability. For decades, the region's identity centered on automotive‚Äîthe assembly plants, the parts suppliers, the engineering firms clustered around Detroit's orbit. That model is fragmenting as vehicles electrify and supply chains reorganize.

But the underlying capability remains. Ontario has skilled machinists, precision manufacturing know-how, and industrial infrastructure that most regions would envy. The question is what we point it toward.

Some of the transition is already happening. Volkswagen's $20 billion EV battery gigafactory in St. Thomas will create thousands of jobs and position Ontario as a key node in the electric vehicle supply chain. That's a meaningful win. But it's also, in some sense, familiar‚Äîa new version of the old model where Ontario makes components for vehicles designed and sold elsewhere.

The more ambitious play is defense manufacturing. Ontario's factories could produce next-generation military equipment: electric armored vehicles, satellite components, advanced materials, autonomous systems. The mechanical engineer in Windsor should be able to imagine designing the next military EV platform, not just the next sedan. That requires deliberate effort to connect the defense tech startup ecosystem with the manufacturing base that could actually build what they design.

Finding Canada's Champion

Ecosystems often need a catalyst‚Äîa visible success that proves what's possible and inspires imitators.

American defense tech had Palmer Luckey, whose company Anduril demonstrated that a startup could build serious military hardware and win major contracts. Whatever one thinks of Luckey personally, Anduril's success unlocked a generation of founders who saw a template and followed it. Success breeds success.

Canada needs an equivalent story. Not necessarily a single individual to lionize, but proof of concept that cutting-edge defense technology can be built on Canadian soil, by Canadian founders, serving Canadian and allied interests. When that story exists, dozens of founders currently on the fence will make different decisions.

This is partly about funding‚ÄîCanada lacks dedicated defense venture funds or an In-Q-Tel equivalent. But it's also about narrative. Few Canadians know about the genuinely impressive work happening at companies like MDA or emerging players like Reaction Dynamics. We're bad at celebrating our own innovators, and that cultural modesty has costs.

Pillar 2: Energy‚ÄîLeveraging Our Power

Energy as Strategy

Energy is the foundation everything else rests on. Economic vitality, military readiness, industrial capacity, and quality of life all depend on reliable, affordable power. For a resource-rich country like Canada, energy represents both strategic advantage and strategic responsibility.

The global context has shifted. For decades, energy policy was primarily an economic and environmental question‚Äîhow to balance development, cost, and emissions. Geopolitics intruded occasionally but could mostly be managed through markets.

That era is ending. Russia's invasion of Ukraine exposed Europe's vulnerability to energy dependence with devastating clarity. Supply chain disruptions during COVID revealed how concentrated critical manufacturing had become. The U.S.-China technology competition has made semiconductors, rare earths, and battery materials into strategic assets subject to export controls and industrial policy.

Energy security is now national security, and Canada is better positioned than almost any country on earth to thrive in this environment.

The Critical Minerals Advantage

Critical minerals are the new oil‚Äîessential inputs for the technologies that will define the coming decades.

Electric vehicles need lithium, cobalt, nickel, and graphite. Semiconductors need gallium and germanium. Clean energy systems need rare earth elements for magnets and specialized alloys. Defense technologies depend on all of the above.

The problem is that China dominates processing and refining for most of these materials. Even when minerals are mined elsewhere, they typically flow through Chinese facilities before reaching manufacturers. This concentration creates strategic vulnerability that Western governments are only beginning to address.

Canada's position is extraordinary. We are the only Western Hemisphere nation possessing all the minerals required for EV batteries. We rank among the top U.S. suppliers for many critical materials. Recent analyses suggest Canada could build the world's leading battery supply chain, potentially overtaking China.

But potential isn't performance. Capturing this opportunity requires action across the value chain: fast-tracking responsible mining projects instead of letting them languish in permitting purgatory, building domestic refining capacity so we process our own materials, and incentivizing downstream manufacturing so the value-add happens here rather than abroad.

The security imperative is clear. These minerals are vital for defense technologies, from fighter jets to guided missiles to secure communications. A supply chain controlled by strategic competitors is a supply chain that can be weaponized against us. By developing our own capacity, Canada can supply itself and allies while reducing dangerous dependencies.

The Nuclear Edge

Ontario has quietly built one of the most impressive nuclear energy systems in the world. Approximately 60% of the province's electricity comes from nuclear plants‚Äîclean, reliable baseload power that has operated safely for decades.

Now Ontario is leading on the next generation of nuclear technology: Small Modular Reactors (SMRs).

The Darlington SMR project will be the first grid-scale SMR in the G7, connecting 300 MW of capacity by 2030. Eventually, four reactors will provide 1,200 MW of carbon-free electricity. The project will create 18,000 construction jobs and add an estimated $35 billion to Ontario's GDP over its lifecycle.

But the strategic significance extends beyond a single power plant. SMRs hit multiple objectives simultaneously: decarbonizing our grid without sacrificing reliability, ensuring energy independence using Canadian uranium, creating a high-skilled industrial workforce in nuclear engineering, and opening export opportunities as other countries seek similar technology.

Success at Darlington positions Canadian firms to export SMR technology worldwide. Countries across Europe, Asia, and the developing world need clean baseload power and lack the expertise to build it themselves. Canada could become a global supplier‚Äînot just of reactors, but of the engineering services, fuel supply, and operational know-how that accompany them.

This is industrial policy at its most strategic: build capability at home, prove it works, then sell it to the world.

A Holistic Energy Strategy

Energy security encompasses Canada's entire resource base, and a serious strategy treats it holistically.

While transitioning toward cleaner energy, our petroleum sector remains economically significant and can fund innovation in carbon capture, hydrogen production, and emissions reduction technologies. Writing off oil and gas entirely would mean abandoning revenue, expertise, and infrastructure that could be redirected toward strategic goals.

Our vast hydroelectric capacity‚Äîespecially in Quebec and British Columbia‚Äîprovides some of the cleanest electricity on earth. This is already attracting energy-intensive industries seeking to reduce their carbon footprint. Data centers, aluminum smelters, and battery manufacturers all want access to abundant clean power.

The key is treating energy as a strategic asset rather than just an economic commodity or environmental problem. A country with plentiful, affordable, clean energy can power industries others can't, attract investment others won't, and maintain sovereignty others lack.

This means hardening infrastructure against physical and cyber threats. It means maintaining strategic reserves. It means developing exportable expertise in areas like battery recycling and portable military power systems. It means regulatory processes that provide certainty and move at the speed investment requires.

Energy abundance is a competitive advantage Canada already possesses. The question is whether we leverage it deliberately or let it dissipate through neglect.

From Vision to Action

The Reinforcing Loop

Talent and Energy aren't separate strategies. They're two halves of a single flywheel.

Cutting-edge energy projects attract talented people. Engineers want to work on first-of-kind SMRs, next-generation battery technology, and critical mineral processing facilities. The problems are hard, the stakes are real, and the work matters. When those opportunities exist in Canada, fewer people leave.

Talented people, in turn, drive innovation in energy and resources. They start companies, improve processes, develop new technologies, and train the next generation. Human capital compounds.

The same logic applies to defense tech. A thriving defense sector attracts engineers and entrepreneurs who want to work on hard problems with national significance. Their presence strengthens the ecosystem, attracts capital, and creates success stories that inspire imitators.

Right now, these loops are running in reverse. Talent leaves because opportunities are elsewhere. Opportunities are elsewhere because talent left. Breaking this cycle requires deliberate intervention‚Äîcreating the initial conditions that let the flywheel start turning in the right direction.

What This Looks Like

Several initiatives could catalyze the shift:

A coordinated effort to recruit Canadian expatriates back home. Thousands of talented Canadians work in Silicon Valley, often in exactly the technical domains‚ÄîAI, hardware, defense‚Äîwhere Canada needs capacity. Many have family ties, cultural affinity, and latent interest in returning if the right opportunity arose. A deliberate program identifying these individuals and matching them with competitive roles, research positions, and startup funding could reverse meaningful portions of the brain drain.

A Canadian Defense Technology Accelerator providing funding, mentorship from military and industry veterans, and fast-track access to DND. Coupled with a government venture fund or In-Q-Tel equivalent, this could seed dozens of companies over five to ten years. The model exists elsewhere. Canada simply hasn't built it.

A cultural narrative shift. Somewhere along the way, Canada became embarrassed about building physical things‚Äîfactories, power plants, military equipment‚Äîas if these were retrograde activities for less enlightened nations. The opposite is true. Advanced manufacturing, energy infrastructure, and defense technology are markers of sophisticated, capable societies. Countries that make things have leverage. Countries that don't are subject to the decisions of those who do.

Policy alignment across the federal government. Procurement reform enabling SMEs to sell to government efficiently. Industrial and Technological Benefits from defense purchases directed toward Canadian startups, not just established primes. Regulatory certainty for mining and nuclear projects, with clear approval timelines that let investment flow.

None of this requires unprecedented budgets or radical policy departures. It requires focus, coordination, and the political will to prioritize Canadian strategic capacity.

Starting Local, Scaling National

Toronto and Southern Ontario represent the natural starting point. The region is Canada's business and technology capital, with a critical mass of talent, capital, and institutions. It also has a manufacturing base actively seeking new direction as automotive transforms.

Success here‚Äîthe first grid-scale SMR at Darlington, battery plants in St. Thomas, a defense tech startup ecosystem taking root‚Äîcreates templates for nationwide replication. Other provinces have different advantages: Quebec's hydropower, Alberta's energy expertise, British Columbia's Pacific trade links. A model that works in Ontario can adapt to these contexts.

But starting somewhere matters more than starting everywhere. Focus creates momentum. Momentum attracts resources. Resources enable scale.

The Mission

This isn't a policy white paper. It's a statement of conviction about what Canada could become.

We have the ingredients for genuine strategic capacity: world-class talent, abundant resources, democratic stability, and alliance relationships that matter. We've simply failed to convert these inputs into outputs. That's a choice, and choices can change.

The goal is becoming a connector and champion‚Äîlinking talent to opportunities, capital to projects, and advancing a simple narrative: Canada can and must build important things. Defense technology. Energy infrastructure. Advanced manufacturing. The hard, physical, consequential work that secures prosperity and sovereignty.

The next generation of Canadians should be able to work on breakthrough technologies without leaving the country. They should see a domestic ecosystem that supports ambition instead of funneling it elsewhere. They should inherit a Canada that builds, not just a Canada that consumes what others have built.

We face real challenges. Arctic security requires capabilities we don't currently possess. Grid stability will determine whether electrification succeeds or stalls. AI development is concentrating in a handful of American and Chinese labs, with Canada watching from the periphery.

But with focused investment in Talent and Energy‚Äîthe two areas where our advantages are clearest‚ÄîCanada can punch above its weight. Not through wishful thinking or national vanity, but through deliberate effort to convert latent potential into strategic reality.

This is the Canadian moment. What we do with it is up to us.

The Work of Getting Up

On being stuck and choosing to move yourself.

Read more

About one month ago, I fell off my motorcycle. Well, fell off is not the most accurate way to put it. Neither is dropped or crashed. I guess I could say I fell with my motorcycle, ending up underneath it.

I was riding up in Northern Ontario. The air was super crisp and the skies were clear. There's nothing like cruising through the side roads smelling the nearby forests.

Honestly, I wasn't going that fast. The sweet spot for my motorcycle is around 70 to 80 km/h. Any more than that and it gets a little buzzy. It was my first long ride, and I was avoiding the major highways for that reason. I chose to take it slower and to finally let my cruiser-style motorcycle go for a proper cruise.

The total journey was about 3 ¬Ω hours. I was already taking longer. I stopped for some breaks here and there. Mostly to re-orient myself as I made a wrong turn (or three). I also wanted to experience silence. It sounds corny, but the quiet rustle of the trees and the absence of any human noise really felt special. I didn't take off my boots and socks and feel the earth between my toes, but the feelings were along those lines.

I first saw the construction signs going into another set of curvy side road. A re-paving project of some kind. Naturally, not a construction worker in sight. Nor anyone else. It was too deserted of a road. It was during a week-day, early afternoon. Perhaps they were taking a break, or working elsewhere. Either way, the signs said to slow down. The first stretch of construction was a smooth, fresh paved road. I had music in my ears and the sun was coming through the trees making a perfect balance against the cool air. I was overconfident, carving through the turns.

There were more warning signs up ahead, and embarrassingly they barely registered in my mind. Then the road surface changed to gravel. Rough and unpaved, the sudden transition startled me. It wouldn't have if I was paying attention and slowed down.

From what I remember, it felt like I was riding over a million tiny speedbumps. I think my right hand tightened on the front brakes instinctively, but unfortunately it was the wrong instinct. By pulling the front breaks and not slowly stepping on the rear brakes, I unbalanced the bike. I don't remember what happened after that.

Not until I was laying up looking at the sky with my motorcycle laying over my left leg, pinning me. The motorcycle was still running but it soon flooded and stopped. My left arm had carved a gouge in the gravel, and that's the first pain that hit me.

I struggled to get up and realized I was stuck. I took my helmet off and tried pulling my leg again. No movement. While the pain in my arm (and the blood) was uncomfortable, another strange feeling came over me. A weird sense of peace and resignation. I was stuck, and it seemed like there was nothing I could do.

I laid my head back down. It was almost like I was taking a rest break.

The gravel was surprisingly comfortable, or maybe I was just too tired to care. My helmet sat a few feet away, the visor scratched. I could hear the wind moving through the trees. No cars. No people. Just me, pinned under 400 pounds of steel and chrome on an empty construction road in Northern Ontario.

I should have been panicking. That's what you're supposed to do, right? But instead, there was this strange clarity. I thought about how I'd probably miss my planned destination. The thing is, no one would have even been waiting for me. I looked at my arm. The scrape was worse than I thought, bleeding steadily into the gravel. I didn't realize how deep a hole one could make in their elbow. Not terrible, but not great either.

The motorcycle's engine had died, but I could smell the fuel. That snapped something in me. Not quite panic, but enough discomfort to try again. I pushed against the bike with my free leg. Nothing. I tried pulling my trapped leg. Still nothing. The weight wasn't crushing, but it was absolute. I was genuinely stuck.

And that's when the resignation deepened. Not into despair exactly, but into a kind of acceptance. Fine, I thought. This is where I am. This is what's happening.

I lay there for what felt like twenty minutes but was probably five. Watching clouds. Listening to nothing. Part of me was waiting for someone to show up‚Äîa construction worker, another rider, anyone. But the other part of me knew I was alone. Really alone. And that this was mine to figure out.

The pain in my arm started to feel less sharp and more insistent. A dull throb that wouldn't let me stay comfortable in my resignation. My leg was starting to go numb under the bike's weight. These weren't emergency-level problems yet, but they were problems that weren't going to solve themselves by lying still.

I took a breath and tried to think practically. The bike was on its left side, pinning my left leg. My right leg was free. Both arms worked, even if one was bleeding. I couldn't lift the bike. Not from this angle, not with this leverage. But maybe I could shift it. Maybe I could wiggle free.

I started small. Pushing the bike with my hands, seeing if I could rock it even an inch. It barely moved, but it moved. I tried again, this time coordinating with my free leg for leverage. Another inch. The pain in my trapped leg sharpened as the pressure shifted, but that meant movement was possible.

It was slow. Pathetically slow. Rock the bike. Rest. Breathe. Push with my leg. Rock again. I was sweating now despite the cool air. My arm was leaving a larger blood stain in the gravel each time I pushed. At some point I started talking to myself‚Äîor maybe to the motorcycle. "Come on. Just a little more."

After what felt like forever, I felt my boot shift. Not free, but looser. I gave one more push, harder this time, and pulled my leg at the same angle. It slid out. Without the boot, but I was free.

I lay there for another minute, this time by choice. My left leg hurt but worked. My arm was a mess. I was covered in dust and blood and some motor oil. But I was out.

Getting up was its own challenge. Everything hurt in new ways once I tried to move. But I stood. The motorcycle lay there like it was taking its own rest break. I checked myself over. Nothing broken. Nothing that couldn't heal. Lucky.

It took me another ten minutes to get the bike upright. It started on the fourth try, as I had flooded the engine. But once started, it seemed like nothing had happened. I sat on it for a moment, not quite ready to ride, just processing. The same road stretched ahead. Still gravel, still unpaved, still marked with warning signs I should have paid more attention to.

I thought about turning back. Taking the long way around. But that felt wrong somehow. Not because I needed to prove something, but because I'd already made it this far. The road would turn back to pavement eventually. I just needed to go slowly. Pay attention. Do the thing I should have done in the first place.

So I did. I rode the rest of that gravel stretch at maybe 30 km/h, standing slightly on the pegs to absorb the bumps, focused entirely on the road surface. When I finally hit pavement again, I didn't speed up right away. I just kept going.

The rest of the ride was unremarkable. I stopped once to clean up my arm at a gas station bathroom. I felt embarrassed. It looked worse than it was. The attendant asked if I was okay. I said yes. He didn't seem convinced, but he didn't push. I don't blame him, I'd be cautious asking too much of a scruffy, blood and dirt covered, tired looking stranger too.

By the time I got back to Toronto, the adrenaline had worn off completely. I was just tired. Sore. My left leg was stiff and the bruise looked gnarly. I was ready to be done for the day.

I've thought about that moment a lot since. Lying there under the bike, feeling stuck, choosing resignation over panic. There was something almost peaceful about it, letting go of control I didn't have anyway. But there was also something dangerous about it. That comfort in being stuck. That ease of just staying down.

Getting out required something different. Not heroic effort or dramatic revelation. Just small movements. Persistent, uncomfortable, incremental pushing until something shifted. And then doing it again. And again.

I'm not going to pretend the motorcycle accident is some grand metaphor for life. But I can't deny the feeling was familiar. That moment of lying still, accepting being stuck, waiting for something external to change. And then the slow realization that nothing was coming. That if I wanted to move, I had to move myself.

The scrapes and bruise healed in about a month. The motorcycle needed some minor work, some scratches I didn't bother fixing.

And I'm still riding, though I slow down for construction zones now.

Easy Friendships, Heavy Burdens

Persona fatigue, and why real relationships are the long game.

When I started working at Canadian Tire‚Äîmy first real job‚ÄîI ended up shadowing this guy named Kevin. He wasn't management, but he ran the floor like it was his. Everyone liked him. He always had a joke ready, remembered customers' names, and could charm a frustrated senior into buying the wrong socket wrench and thanking him for it.

But here's the thing: Kevin didn't actually know that much about tools. He'd ask me questions about SKUs that I, a 17-year-old barely trained seasonal hire, had to guess my way through. But it didn't matter. Kevin understood people‚Äîand in retail, that's 90% of the job.

He couldn't explain how he did it. You just had to watch. I did.

From Kevin, I learned how to throw compliments like darts‚Äîfast, effortless, and mostly accurate. How a little physical presence‚Äîlike walking beside someone or handing them the item they were looking for‚Äîcould build micro-trust. How not to challenge people's assumptions, but to lean into their version of the story.

He taught me that you didn't need to be good all the time‚Äîjust memorable in the moments that mattered.

The result? Kevin could sell snow shovels in July. He was magnetic. And I wanted to be like him.

So I learned the tricks. I learned how to manage a room, how to read moods and mirror them, how to switch gears depending on who I was with. And it worked. It worked in retail, it worked in interviews, it even worked at parties.

But when it came to actual friendship‚Äîreal, lasting, show-up-when-it's-bad friendship‚Äîit fell apart.

The truth is, you can't game long-haul relationships. You can't endlessly adapt and expect depth to grow. If you're constantly tweaking yourself to be more appealing, you're also telling yourself: who I really am isn't enough.

And worse, it's exhausting.

There are people in your life‚Äîmaybe just one or two‚Äîwho always make you feel like you have to be "on." Where hanging out feels like a low-stakes audition. Where you rehearse your stories a bit more, where your shirt fits a little tighter. And maybe at first, it feels like winning. You're in. You're admired.

But you do it 10 times. Then 50. Then 100. And suddenly the whole thing feels like emotional cardio: high effort, low reward.

Every time you shift a little to fit someone's frame, you add friction. It's tiny, at first‚Äîjust a sliver of self-betrayal. But friction adds up. One day, you'll choose ease over effort. You'll stop texting back. You'll ghost the group chat. And you'll tell yourself you're just "too busy." But really, you're just tired of shape-shifting.

What I've come to believe is that the real killer of meaningful relationships isn't distance or disagreement‚Äîit's the slow, subtle erosion of self over time. Because eventually, we all run out of stamina for pretending.

Imagine climbing a mountain with a backpack full of water bottles that aren't yours. That's what curated relationships feel like. You think you're being generous. But you're just making the climb harder, and lonelier.

The people worth building with‚Äîthe ones you actually want around in ten years‚Äîwon't need the theatrics. They'll want your messy, quiet, smart-ass, distracted, ridiculous self. Not your "customer-facing" version.

I used to think Kevin was winning. But eventually I noticed he spent most of his shift in the stockroom, scrolling his phone, cracking jokes with whoever wandered back. That charisma wasn't free‚Äîit came with a price. And downtime was how he paid for it.

He was recovering. From the weight of the role he had to play.

I've played that role too. For a long time. I wanted to be impressive, magnetic, indispensable. I wanted people to think, "Damn, I need that guy around." And maybe they did‚Äîfor a while. But it wore me down. Because the second you build a relationship on the performance, you're stuck performing just to keep it.

Real connection is quiet. It's who still shows up when there's nothing left to impress.

So now? I'm aiming for friendships where I don't have to rehearse. Where I don't need a costume. Where being me is‚Äîsomehow‚Äîstill enough.

The Strength in Seeing

Small moments of care that shape us.

Every so often, you catch it. Someone close to breaking. It's not loud or dramatic. There's no scene. Just a small fracture at the edge of their face, the kind we've all carried at some point. They might be sitting a little too still, or speaking in a tone that feels almost too careful, as if any sudden movement might cause the whole thing to crack open.

It's subtle enough that you could miss it entirely if you weren't paying attention. And most people do.

In those moments, you face a choice. You can look away, because that's the easier thing to do. The thing we've been trained for. You can give them their space, pretend you didn't notice, tell yourself you'd only make it worse by stepping in. We convince ourselves that's the polite option, that it's what they would want.

Or you can do the harder thing.

Not the big, dramatic, world-saving thing. Just the quiet, human act of not letting someone carry the full weight of their invisibility. That doesn't have to mean a grand gesture. It might simply mean holding their gaze for a moment longer than you normally would. Or saying something small and soft, in a way no one else can hear. Or offering a resource, a nudge, a reminder that they're not completely alone‚Äîeven though you may never know whether it helped.

Because you don't know what people are carrying. You don't know how close someone might be to breaking. And you may never know what difference it makes when you choose to notice. But it does make a difference.

I think about these moments long after they pass. The person on the bench. The colleague whose words sounded a little too measured, as if they might splinter if pushed even slightly. The friend at dinner who pushed food around their plate and smiled at all the right times but never truly joined the conversation. I may not know the full story of what they were carrying, but I do know I was able to offer something small. A word, a resource, a quiet moment of acknowledgment‚Äîand I hope it lightened things for them, even just a little.

It's not about changing the world. It's not about a lesson. It's about one person noticing another, just long enough for them to feel less alone. That's all.

Because strength isn't always about holding your own line. Sometimes it's about holding the line for someone else, even for just a moment, without them ever having to ask.

That's not about being a savior. It's about being human.

And the truth is, those moments don't just help the other person. They shape us too. They remind us that the people around us are more than passing shapes in a crowd. They remind us that we are all, at different times, the one holding it together by a thread.

Maybe that's the quiet purpose of it all. To be the kind of person who notices. To step just slightly outside the cultural script that tells us to stay separate. To take the risk of showing a small act of care, even when it would be easier not to.

Because we never know when that one small choice might mean more than we can imagine.
