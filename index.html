The truth is, you can't game long-haul relationships. You can't endlessly adapt and expect depth to grow. If you're constantly tweaking yourself to be more appealing, you're also telling yourself: who I really am isn't enough.

Every time you shift a little to fit someone's frame, you add friction. It's tiny, at first‚Äîjust a sliver of self-betrayal. But friction adds up. One day, you'll choose ease over effort. The people worth building with‚Äîthe ones you actually want around in ten years‚Äîwon't need the theatrics. They'll want your messy, quiet, smart-ass, distracted, ridiculous self. Not your "customer-facing" version.

The Quiet Permission of Walkabouts

On stepping away to return to yourself

Read more

I've always needed a way to quietly leave. Not escape‚Äîjust reset. This piece is about the walkabouts I take when I need to be nobody for a little while. Not a productivity hack. Not a routine. Just a quiet return to myself.

There's a certain rhythm I've had to relearn‚Äîone that isn't scheduled, optimized, or announced. It's not something I talk about often, because it doesn't lend itself to a calendar invite or a thread. But it's shaped everything that matters.

I call them walkabouts.

Not the kind with hiking boots or backpacks or photo evidence. Not exercise. Not even "getting steps in." My walkabouts are quieter than that. More selfish. They are when I leave‚Äînot for long, but long enough. Out the door. Down a street. Sometimes nowhere. Sometimes somewhere very specific. A bench. A railing. A stoop that catches the light.

And I just walk.

These are not breaks from work. They are breaks from identity. From the version of myself that's required‚Äîon calls, in family rooms, in messages, in roles. There are days when I need to stop holding my life up like it's a tray I'm carrying with careful balance, and instead set it down beside me and look at it without needing to perform inside it.

That's what a walkabout gives me: a chance to step outside the story I'm in, without needing to end the story entirely.

I started doing this in moments of emotional crisis. Days when I couldn't hear myself think inside the house. Nights when I was a little too known by the people I loved. I'd say, "I need to go for a quick walk." Sometimes I wouldn't even say it. Just go.

And I noticed something. After ten minutes, I was no longer thinking about what had happened. I wasn't even thinking about who I was supposed to be. I was seeing things again. A light bouncing off a window. The precise cruelty of a dead branch on a perfect tree. The teenager yelling into his headphones. The brief permission to feel like nobody in particular.

That anonymity made space for something else to emerge: a kind of slow inner honesty that doesn't show up in meetings or journal pages. The truth that waits for silence. The part of me that doesn't interrupt. That just‚Ä¶ watches.

And it didn't take long for that to become essential.

I don't know how to explain this without sounding like I'm romanticizing loneliness. But walkabouts aren't lonely. They're private. They're intimate in a way that's hard to describe‚Äîlike slipping into bed alone and knowing your body again. Like returning to a part of yourself that doesn't need translation.

The best ones don't solve anything. They don't end with a conclusion. There's no big insight, no pivot, no profound decision. Just a kind of cellular reset. Like my mind sighs. Like I can come back inside without dragging all my shadows behind me.

We don't talk about that enough, do we? The small, unsponsored rituals that keep people sane. The ones that don't scale. That don't get published or posted or tracked. The sacred habits that don't look impressive but quietly hold your life together.

For me, it's walking.

It's not a routine. It's a returning.

It's the thing that reminds me I exist outside of anyone's expectations, outside of productivity, outside of even who I've told myself I am. A walkabout is not a retreat. It's a re-becoming. And I need it. More than I admit.

So if you ever see me stepping out without warning‚Äîdon't ask where I'm going. I won't know until I get there. And I won't be gone long.

But when I come back, I'll be a little more myself again.
